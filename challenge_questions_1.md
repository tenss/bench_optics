# Challenge Questions [Set 1]

A good answer does three things:
1. **Explains how the technique works**: Build the explanation from the optical principles we have covered.
2. **Connects it to the course**: Name the underlying ideas (e.g., refraction, focal length, numerical aperture, diffraction) and show how the technique exploits or works around them.

> **A word of caution**: Be wary of diagrams you find online: many are wrong, oversimplified. Reason from first principles and from the lecture material.

## 1. A thin, short-focal-length lens
Large-diameter lenses, especially with short focal lengths, can become quite bulky and heavy.

- Explain how a lens could focus light while having the dimensions of a credit card: flat and thin.
- Draw the **ray diagram** and use the concepts we discussed.

## 2. Mirage
Explain how a mirage works. For example, when you see cars "reflected" on a hot road.

- Use concepts we discussed.
- Are these mechanisms or principles ever used in neuroscience?

## 3. Pixel density
The screen of your phone is a collection of tiny pixels. Different screen models are often marketed through their pixel density. Are they telling the truth?

Measure the pixel density of your phone using a laser pointer.

- Describe how you achieved it using the concepts discussed in the course.
- Are the manufacturer's specs accurate?

## 4. The eye
Explain the eye as an optical system.

- What is its resolution? What is the **NA** of the lens?
- What goes wrong in near- and far-sighted eyes, and how do glasses correct for it?
- What is astigmatism?
- Why do people squint to see something better?
