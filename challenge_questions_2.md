# Challenge questions [Set 2]

A good answer does three things:

1. **Explains how the technique works**: Build the explanation from the optical principles we have covered.
2. **Connects it to the course**: Name the underlying ideas (e.g. diffraction limit, conjugate planes) and show how the technique exploits or works around them.
3. **States when it gives an advantage**: Under what experimental conditions (e.g., sample type, required resolution, speed, depth, photodamage) you would actually reach for this method instead of a simpler one, and what you trade away to get there.

> **A word of caution**: Be wary of diagrams you find online: many are wrong, oversimplified. Reason from first principles and from the lecture material.

## 1. Superresolution

How do the superresolution techniques **PALM** and **STORM** work? 

- Explain **STED**, and in particular how the illumination patterns are generated.
- What is the source of the resolution improvement, and in which experimental situation would you choose one of these over the others?
- What about **expansion microscopy**?

## 2. Spinning disc

How does a **spinning disc confocal** microscope work?

- Draw the **ray diagram**, and explain how it achieves optical sectioning. 
- When does it offer an advantage over a standard point-scanning confocal?

## 3. Wavefront sensor

How does a **Shack–Hartmann wavefront sensor** work?

- Use **ray diagrams** and concepts from the lectures. 
- Make clear what quantity is actually being measured at the detector and how an aberrated wavefront is reconstructed from it.
- How would you use it to beat scattering-induced deformations of your PSF?

## 4. Remote focusing

Moving your sample or your objective is lame. Can you think of an alternative way to adjust your plane of focus quickly?

- Draw the **ray diagram** for such a device.
